## Area D · Cookie banner and CMP: design and behavior

**Applies if**: the entire Area D applies only if the site installs or reads cookies and other non-technical tracking tools (non-anonymized analytics, marketing, profiling, social plugins, third-party trackers) that require consent under Art. 5(3) Directive 2002/58/EC [N3] and Art. 122 of D.lgs. 196/2003 [N2]. A site with only technical or strictly necessary cookies does not require the banner and these checks do not apply.

**Rationale**: the banner is the interface of consent. If Area C verifies what fires technically before consent, Area D verifies whether the consent collected is valid: freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous (Art. 4(11) GDPR). A poorly designed banner is not an aesthetic flaw, it is a defect that invalidates upstream all the consents collected and therefore makes unlawful every tracker switched on that basis. The requirements are precise and convergent: the cookie guidelines of the Garante (the Italian DPA) of 10 June 2021 (doc-web 9677876), the EDPB guidelines 03/2022 on deceptive design patterns [S47] and the practice of the other EU authorities (the CNIL, the French DPA, which here serves as comparative reference and which has already issued formal notices to publishers precisely over the dark patterns of banners [S50]) say the same things: refusal at the first level with equal prominence, no pre-checked boxes, no equating of silence/scroll/closing with consent, no graphic or textual manipulation. The guiding rule is prohibited asymmetry: accepting and refusing must cost the same.

**Legal bases**: Art. 4(11) GDPR (definition of consent: freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous), Art. 7 GDPR (conditions for consent: burden of proof, withdrawal under Art. 7(3), consent not conditional under Art. 7(4)), Art. 5(1)(a) GDPR (fairness and transparency), Art. 12(1) GDPR (concise information and clear language), Art. 13 GDPR (information notice), Art. 25(2) GDPR (data protection by default), Art. 5(3) Directive 2002/58/EC ePrivacy [N3], Art. 122 D.lgs. 196/2003 Codice Privacy (Italian Privacy Code) [N2], Recitals 32, 42, 43 GDPR [N1]; Guidelines of the Garante on cookies and other tracking tools, 10 June 2021, doc-web 9677876 [G1]; EDPB Guidelines 05/2020 on consent [E2]; EDPB Guidelines 03/2022 on deceptive design patterns [E3, S47]; EDPB Opinion 08/2024 on consent-or-pay [E4]; CJEU C-673/17 Planet49 [J1]; CNIL formal notice on dark patterns in cookie banners, referencing the EDPB final report on cookie banners of 17 January 2023 [S50]. The items rely on the authority's checklist [S5] and, for breadth, on vendor/community checklists [S9, S10, S11], never on their own as a foundation.

| D-03 | The banner's close X and the mere continuation of browsing are NOT treated as consent | Consent requires a positive and unambiguous act (Art. 4(11), Recital 32 GDPR): silence, closing the banner and continuing to browse amount to a refusal, not consent (CJEU C-673/17 Planet49 [J1]; Garante Guidelines doc-web 9677876 [G1]). Principle established by the CJEU in Planet49 (C-673/17) [J1]; the EDPB catalogs it as deceptive snugness, whereby the most invasive option must never be pre-selected [S47] [S5, S9, S10, S11] | MANDATORY | HIGH | 🟢 | Inspection of the preferences panel DOM: `checked` attribute on checkboxes and `on`/`aria-checked` state on the toggles of non-technical categories; flag every pre-activated control |
| D-05 | Granularity per purpose in the second level, with independent toggles for each category ([COND: IAB TCF framework] also per single vendor) | Consent must be specific (Art. 4(11), Recital 43 GDPR): the user must be able to accept analytics and reject marketing separately. A single "all or nothing" switch in the panel does not satisfy specificity. Where the site adopts IAB TCF, granularity must also be guaranteed per single vendor [S5, S10, S11] | MANDATORY | MEDIUM | 🟢 | Counting and classification of the toggles in the preferences panel (strictly necessary, functional, analytics, marketing); flag if there is a single cumulative control or if categories corresponding to the trackers detected in Area C are missing |
| D-06 | No cookie wall: the site content is accessible even without giving consent to non-technical trackers ([COND: presence of a paid alternative] consent-or-pay models must be assessed separately) | Making access to content conditional on accepting trackers makes consent not freely given (Art. 7(4) GDPR; Recital 43). Consent-or-pay models (paying as an alternative to consent) are allowed only under strict conditions and require a dedicated case-by-case assessment (EDPB Opinion 08/2024 [E4]). The foundation lies in the guidelines and in the EDPB guidance, an area still under debate | RECOMMENDED | HIGH | 🟡 | Navigation with the banner ignored/closed: verify whether the content remains usable or whether a blocking overlay prevents access in the absence of consent; report any paid alternatives for the consent-or-pay analysis |
| D-07 | No scroll-consent: scrolling the page does not activate trackers nor is it equated with consent | Scrolling the page is not an unambiguous manifestation of will: the Garante Guidelines of 10 June 2021 [G1] expressly exclude scroll as a mode of collecting consent. A script that switches on trackers on scroll nullifies the positive act required by Art. 4(11) and violates Art. 5(3) ePrivacy [N3] and Art. 122 Codice [N2] [S5] | MANDATORY | CRITICAL | 🟢 | Programmatic scroll of the page without any click on the banner, then repetition of checks C-01..C-05: if scrolling makes non-technical trackers fire, the finding is confirmed |
| D-08 | Withdrawal of consent as easy as giving it: persistent preferences management widget/link reachable from every page (typically in the footer) | Art. 7(3) GDPR requires that withdrawing consent be as simple as giving it, and that the data subject be informed of this before collection (Recital 42). A stable and always-available access point is needed (floating icon or "Cookie preferences" link in the footer) that reopens the choice panel; if consent is given with one click, withdrawal must not require more steps [S47]. Also relevant is respect for automated signals such as Global Privacy Control [S5, S10, S11] | MANDATORY | HIGH | 🟢 | Multi-page crawl looking for a preferences management link/widget (text such as "Cookie preferences", "Manage consent", CMP reopening); verify that it actually reopens the panel; check compliance with the GPC/DNT header where declared |
| D-09 | The banner is not re-proposed in a nagging way after a refusal (Garante and CNIL guidance: retain the choice for at least 6 months, save for significant changes to the processing) | Re-proposing the banner at every visit to someone who has already refused makes the refusal burdensome and pushes toward acceptance out of exhaustion: the EDPB catalogs it as continuous prompting, the repeated request that wears the user down until they give in, in conflict with Art. 7 and Art. 4(11) GDPR [S47]. The choice, whether consent or refusal, must be retained for a reasonable period indicated by the Garante and CNIL as at least 6 months, save for significant changes to the processing (practice of the authorities) [S5] | RECOMMENDED | MEDIUM | 🟡 | Simulation of multiple successive sessions after a refusal: verify that the banner does not reappear with a request to choose within the expected time frame; inspection of the duration of the cookie/record that stores the refusal choice |
| D-10 | Non-manipulative wording and design: no guilt-tripping, false urgency, misleading linguistic or chromatic hierarchies, confusing texts | Consent obtained with dark patterns (faded buttons for refusal, language that shames those who refuse, artificial urgency, double-negative formulations) is neither freely given nor informed. Fairness (Art. 5(1)(a) GDPR) is the umbrella test: a deceptive pattern constitutes unfair processing even if it respects the other principles, and undermines the validity of consent (Art. 4(11)) [S47]. The EDPB taxonomy of deceptive patterns (overloading, skipping, stirring, obstructing, fickle, left in the dark) is the reference for the verdict [E3, S47]; the CNIL challenges designs that surreptitiously steer the choice toward acceptance [S50] [S5] | RECOMMENDED | HIGH | 🟡 | Extraction of the banner's texts and styles + LLM verdict on the EDPB taxonomy of deceptive patterns (overloading, skipping, stirring, obstructing, fickle, left in the dark) |
| D-11 | Short information notice at the first level of the banner (purposes in summary) with a link to the extended cookie notice: two-tier information | The banner must inform before collecting (Art. 12(1), Art. 13 GDPR; Art. 122 Codice [N2]): a comprehensible summary of the purposes at the first level and a link to the detailed cookie policy at the second. The CNIL requires clear and complete information that specifies the purposes of the cookies and the concrete means to refuse them [S50]. A banner with only "Accept", without explanation or link, does not make consent informed [S5, S10] | MANDATORY | MEDIUM | 🟢 | Verify the presence in the banner of a summary text on the purposes and of a working link (HTTP 200) to the extended cookie policy and to the updated list of processors/third parties |
| D-12 | The banner is present and compliant also on mobile viewport (same guarantees of parity, granularity and readability as desktop) | The mobile experience is often different: reduced banners, "Reject" pushed off-screen or behind scroll, overlapping buttons, preferences panel that cannot be opened. The same rules D-02..D-05 apply identically on mobile; the EDPB requires particular care for consent on mobile interfaces and consistency of the interface across devices (Art. 7(2), Art. 12(1) GDPR) [S47]. The guarantees of parity, granularity and readability are binding regardless of the viewport | MANDATORY | HIGH | 🟢 | Re-running the D-01..D-05 scan with mobile viewport and emulation (user-agent, touch): comparison of button parity and preferences panel accessibility relative to desktop |
| D-13 | The user's choices (consent or refusal) are stored and not re-solicited on every page or on every new nearby visit | Distinct from D-09 (reappearance after a refusal in the medium term): here we verify that the expressed choice is retained and respected during and between sessions, without re-presenting the banner on every page browsed. Re-presenting it continuously is both undue pressure and evidence that the choice is not respected (Art. 5(3) ePrivacy [N3], Art. 7 GDPR). Includes respect for automated signals (GPC/DNT) where declared, which in the Italian legal system remain a recommendation and not an obligation [S5, S11] | RECOMMENDED | MEDIUM | 🟢 | Navigation of multiple pages in the same session after expressing a choice + a second nearby session: verify that the banner does not reappear with a request to choose and that the tracker state remains consistent with the stored choice; inspection of the cookie/record of the choice state |
| D-14 | A global consent ("Accept all" with one click) is allowed only if all purposes are presented to the user BEFORE that click | Bulk consent is not prohibited, but it is valid only if informed and specific (Art. 4(11), Art. 13 GDPR): the user must be able to know the set of purposes before accepting them all together. An "Accept all" that groups purposes not listed at the first level nor accessible before the click is neither informed nor specific and therefore invalid [S5] | MANDATORY | HIGH | 🟡 | Inspection of the first level of the banner: verify that the purposes (or the tracking categories) are listed or immediately accessible before the "Accept all" button; flag if global consent precedes the presentation of the purposes |
| D-15 | Refusal button label explicit and unambiguous ("Reject", "Reject all"), not misleading or conditioned formulations | Consent and refusal must be informed and comprehensible (Art. 4(11), Art. 12(1) GDPR): an ambiguous label does not make clear to the user the effect of the click. The CNIL explicitly cites formulations such as "I decline non-essential purposes" as an example of non-explicit and misleading terminology that hinders free choice [S50] | RECOMMENDED | MEDIUM | 🟡 | Extraction of the text label of the banner buttons + LLM verdict on clarity: flag if the refusal button uses ambiguous, conditioned or non-explicit formulations instead of a clear "Reject"/"Reject all" |
| D-16 | The "Accept" button does not appear multiple times in the banner while the "Reject" appears only once | Multiplying the occasions to accept while showing the refusal only once is an imbalance that steers the choice and compromises the freedom of consent (Art. 4(11) GDPR): the CNIL qualifies as a dark pattern the repetition of the acceptance button against a single refusal button [S50] | RECOMMENDED | MEDIUM | 🟡 | Counting in the DOM of the acceptance and refusal controls at the first level and in the preferences panel; flag if the occurrences of "Accept" exceed those of "Reject" |
| D-17 | Banner and cookie notice in the language of the service and of the user (for a site aimed at the Italian public, in Italian) | Information on the processing must be intelligible, in clear and plain language (Art. 12(1) GDPR): the EDPB catalogs as language discontinuity the provision of the information notice in a language different from that of the service or of the user's country, which prevents its reading and undermines informed consent (Art. 13, Art. 14 GDPR) [S47]. A banner only in English on a site in Italian does not make consent informed | RECOMMENDED | MEDIUM | 🟡 | Automatic detection of the language of the banner text and of the notice link and comparison with the prevailing language of the site (`lang` attribute, content): flag any mismatch |
| D-18 | Neutral explanation of the consequences when the user activates/deactivates a control or gives/withdraws consent | EDPB best practice (explaining consequences): informing neutrally about the consequences of each choice fosters a genuinely aware consent consistent with the "No deception" principle, though without constituting a legal obligation (Art. 12(1), Art. 5(1)(a) GDPR) [S47] | PLUS | LOW | 🔴 | Manual verification of the presence, next to the toggles of the preferences panel, of a brief neutral description of the effect of activating/deactivating each category |

**Client questions (Area D)**

**CMP and configuration**
- Which CMP (Consent Management Platform) do you use: Iubenda, OneTrust, Cookiebot, CookieYes, Usercentrics, custom solution? Who configures it and who keeps it up to date? (relevant for the entire Area D)
- Has the banner been customized relative to the CMP's default template? By whom (agency, in-house, developer) and with what legal review? (CMP defaults are not always compliant with the Garante)
- Does the CMP actually block scripts "upstream" until the user chooses, or does it merely display a notice while the trackers fire anyway? (D-01, cross-reference with Area C)

**Behavior and design**
- Have you ever A/B tested the banner or optimized for the acceptance rate? With what metrics? (optimizing to make people accept is an indicator of dark pattern risk, D-02 and D-10)
- Is the "Reject all" button present at the first level of the banner with the same prominence as "Accept all", also on mobile? How is it labeled? (D-02, D-12, D-15)
- Does the "Accept" button appear only once or is it repeated in multiple places of the banner compared to "Reject"? (D-16)
- What happens if the user closes the banner with the X or continues browsing without choosing: do the trackers fire anyway? (D-03)

**Storage, withdrawal and signals**
- Where and for how long do you retain the user's choice (consent and refusal)? Does the banner reappear at every visit or do you respect a retention period for the choice (6-month guidance)? (D-09, D-13)
- Is there a permanent access point (link or icon in the footer) to reopen the preferences and withdraw consent at any time? (D-08)
- Do you respect automated signals such as Global Privacy Control (GPC) or Do Not Track (DNT) sent by the browser? (D-08, D-13)

**Information and language**
- Does the banner show a summary of the purposes at the first level with a link to the extended cookie policy, and specify the concrete means to refuse? (D-11)
- In which languages are the banner and notice available? Are they consistent with the language of the site and of the public you address? (D-17)

**Access and business models**
- Is the site content accessible also to those who refuse the trackers, or is there a block (cookie wall)? Do you offer a paid alternative to consent (consent-or-pay model)? (D-06)
- Do you adopt the IAB TCF (Transparency and Consent Framework) with a vendor list? If so, is consent granularity managed also per single vendor? (D-05)
