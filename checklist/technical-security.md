## Area F · Technical security (Art. 32)

**Rationale**: the security of processing is an autonomous obligation that must be adequate to the risk: formal compliance of the notices is not enough, personal data must be protected with concrete technical and organisational measures. For a website and for the systems that feed it, this means an encrypted channel everywhere, a modern TLS configuration, security headers, protected cookies, no exposed sensitive files or information, but also encryption of data at rest and of backups, robust password hashing, least-privilege access controls with tracking, timely patching and pseudonymisation where possible. The externally observable surface is entirely automatable: encryption at rest, password management, access controls and patching are instead verified through documentation with the client, because they concern the back-end and the processes. Security must be measured on the data, not on the declaration.

**Legal bases**: Art. 32 GDPR (security of processing, measures adequate to the risk: encryption, pseudonymisation, confidentiality, integrity, availability and resilience of systems, restoration capability, periodic testing and verification) [N1], Art. 32(1)(a) (encryption and pseudonymisation) [N1], Art. 32(1)(b) (confidentiality, integrity, availability) [N1], Art. 32(1)(c) (ability to restore availability and access) [N1], Art. 32(1)(d) (periodic testing and verification of effectiveness) [N1], Art. 5(1)(f) GDPR (integrity and confidentiality) [N1], Art. 25 GDPR (data protection by design and by default) [N1], Art. 39 GDPR (training and awareness as an organisational component of security) [N1]. Methodological and classificatory support for the technical surface: the TLS/SSL and security header sheets of the EDPB Website Auditing Tool [S4, S6], the Website Evidence Collector by the EDPS [S7], technical control checklists [S1, S2, S3, S10, S11].

| F-08 | No sensitive files exposed: .git, .env, backups, dumps, directory listing | Direct exposure of repositories, configuration files, backups or dumps is an immediate leak of data or credentials, among the most serious and most trivial breaches of Art. 32 (probe not present in the corpus). | F-09 | 404/error pages that do not reveal stack traces or software versions | Stack traces and version banners are information disclosure: they ease the identification of known vulnerabilities of the CMS, framework or plugins. A standard hardening measure in support of Art. 32 GDPR [N1] (probe not present in the corpus) | RECOMMENDED | LOW | 🟢 | Probe of a non-existent page and analysis of the response body searching for stack traces, absolute paths, software names and versions [S4] |
| F-10 | Domain email: SPF, DKIM, DMARC configured | A domain without email authentication is spoofable and becomes a phishing vector toward the data subjects themselves, with an impact on the confidentiality of their data. A standard organisational measure in support of Art. 32 GDPR [N1] (DNS verification not present in the corpus) | RECOMMENDED | MEDIUM | 🟢 | DNS TXT query on the SPF, DKIM (known selectors) and DMARC records, verifying syntax and policy (p=none/quarantine/reject) |
| F-11 | [COND: login area present] Login form: no evident user enumeration, perceivable rate limiting | Error messages that distinguish "non-existent user" from "wrong password" enable account enumeration; the absence of rate limiting opens the way to credential stuffing and brute force. Technical practice (starting point from [S2]) with regular audits and penetration tests recommended by [S11], in support of Art. 32 GDPR [N1] | RECOMMENDED | MEDIUM | 🟡 | Light, non-intrusive test (2-3 requests): comparison of the messages and response times between an existing and a non-existent user; observation of any block after repeated failed attempts |
| F-12 | Encryption of personal data at rest (AES-256) and of backups with the same robustness | Encryption at rest protects databases, file stores and backups in case of unauthorised access or theft of the medium: it is the measure that makes a breach non-communicable to the data subjects when the data is unreadable to third parties. It must extend to backups, which replicate the same personal data. The norm indicates it as a measure appropriate to the risk, not as an absolute obligation. Art. 32(1)(a) GDPR (encryption) [N1], Art. 32(1)(b) (confidentiality) [N1]; cf. Art. 34(3)(a) on the exemption from communication if the data was encrypted [N1] | RECOMMENDED | HIGH | 🔴 | Not observable from the outside: documentary verification at intake (encryption of the database, file store and backups; algorithm and key management). Flag as a point to verify with the client [S1, S10, S11] |
| F-13 | Password hashing with bcrypt/scrypt/Argon2 (never in the clear, never with fast unsalted hashes) | Passwords stored in the clear or with weak digests (MD5, unsalted SHA-1) are compromised en masse at the first unauthorised access, with a cascade effect on credential reuse. Storage in the clear is a direct and repeatedly sanctioned breach; the choice of a slow, salted algorithm is the required standard. Art. 32 GDPR [N1], Art. 5(1)(f) [N1] | MANDATORY | CRITICAL | 🔴 | Not observable from the outside: documentary/code verification at intake on the hashing algorithm used for account credentials. Indirect indicator: a password recovery flow that sends the old password in the clear reveals unhashed storage [S11] |
| F-14 | Least-privilege access controls and logging of access to personal data | Access to data must be limited to those who need it for the purpose (need-to-know) and every access, particularly administrative access, must be tracked: without logs an incident cannot be reconstructed nor can accountability be demonstrated. Most breaches involve internal access, whether legitimate or compromised. Access control is a binding component of confidentiality. Art. 32 GDPR [N1], Art. 32(1)(b) [N1], Art. 5(2) accountability [N1] | MANDATORY | HIGH | 🔴 | Not observable from the outside: documentary verification at intake on the roles and permissions model (least-privilege principle), presence and retention of access logs to data and to administrative functions, MFA on privileged accounts [S2, S10, S11] |
| F-15 | Patching process with a defined SLA (critical security patches within 48 hours) | An unupdated CMS, framework or plugin is the most exploited entry point: security is adequate only if known vulnerabilities are closed within certain and verifiable timeframes, not ad hoc. Formalising the SLA is an organisational practice in support of Art. 32 GDPR [N1], Art. 32(1)(d) (periodic verification of the effectiveness of measures) [N1] | RECOMMENDED | HIGH | 🔴 | Not observable with certainty from the outside: documentary verification at intake on the existence of a patching process with an SLA (window for critical patches, responsible party, tracking). Indirect indicator: the detected software versions (F-04, F-09) are known to be vulnerable [S11] |
| F-16 | Pseudonymisation/anonymisation of personal data where technically possible | Pseudonymisation and anonymisation are measures explicitly indicated by the norm to reduce the risk and impact of a breach: pseudonymised data is less exposed, anonymised data leaves the GDPR perimeter entirely. They should be applied by default in the design of processing, as an appropriate measure and not as an absolute obligation. Art. 32(1)(a) GDPR (pseudonymisation) [N1], Art. 25 GDPR (data protection by design and by default) [N1] | RECOMMENDED | MEDIUM | 🔴 | Not observable from the outside: documentary verification at intake on where data is pseudonymised or anonymised (e.g. analytics, logs, test and development environments, reporting datasets) and on how the separation of the additional information enabling re-identification is managed [S1, S10, S11] |

**Engagement note**: checks F-08 and F-11 must be carried out only with the client's written authorisation to the assessment. No intrusive testing beyond passive probes without an explicit mandate. Checks F-12, F-13, F-14, F-15 and F-16 concern the back-end and the processes: they are not observable from the web scan and must be verified through documentation at intake.

**Client questions (Area F)**

**Infrastructure and patching**
- Who manages the infrastructure (in-house, agency, managed hosting)? Who has privileged access to the servers and the database?
- Is there a patching process for the CMS, the framework and the plugins? With what SLA for critical security patches (e.g. within 48 hours) and who is responsible for it?
- Does the site serve fonts from an external CDN (remote Google Fonts) or host them locally? Does it use a third-party CAPTCHA on login and forms?

**Encryption, backup and minimisation**
- Is personal data encrypted at rest (database, file store)? With what algorithm (e.g. AES-256) and how are the keys managed? Does the same encryption apply to backups?
- Where are backups stored, in how many locations, and how often are restores tested? Is the deletion of a piece of data propagated to the backups as well, and within what timeframe?
- Where do you apply pseudonymisation or anonymisation (analytics, logs, test and development environments, reporting)? How is the separation of the information enabling re-identification managed?

**Passwords and access controls**
- How are the passwords of user and administrator accounts stored (hashing algorithm: bcrypt, scrypt, Argon2)? Is multi-factor authentication active on privileged accounts?
- Is there a least-privilege roles and permissions model? Are access to data and administrative operations tracked in logs, and for how long are they retained?

**Incidents, audits and training**
- Have you ever had a data breach or a security incident? Was it notified to the Garante (the Italian DPA) under Art. 33 and, if required, communicated to the data subjects under Art. 34?
- Do you carry out periodic security audits or penetration tests? At what cadence and by whom?
- Does the staff that processes personal data receive training on data protection (Art. 32 and Art. 39)?
