## Area L · E-commerce

**Applies if**: the entire area applies only if the site sells goods or services online (checkout with payment, catalog with cart, paid subscriptions). If the site does not conclude online sales, the area is out of scope and its checks are not run.

**Rationale**: e-commerce concentrates the most sensitive processing on the web, often on a single data subject and at the same moment: identification of the buyer, shipping and billing address, payment data, purchase history, commercial profiling, promotional communications. The difficulty is not the presence of this data but the layering of different legal bases on the same subject and sometimes on the same datum: the sales contract (Art. 6(1)(b)) covers order fulfillment and delivery, the legal obligation (Art. 6(1)(c)) requires keeping the invoice well beyond the end of the relationship, consent (Art. 6(1)(a)) is needed to save the card, to profile, or to send marketing that goes beyond soft spam, legitimate interest (Art. 6(1)(f)) supports fraud prevention but does not support imposing an account. The recurring error is to treat the shop as a single container in which, once the customer is acquired, everything is permitted: collecting more than what is needed to invoice and ship, imposing registration to buy, saving the card by default, reusing the order email for unrelated campaigns, keeping in an active database data that should live only in the tax archive. Each purpose must be isolated, given its own legal basis and its own retention period. The perimeter of this area is what is specific to e-commerce and not already covered elsewhere: the legal basis of the account and the services that can be provided without an account, the payment datum and the perimeter of the PSP, post-purchase marketing (soft spam and abandoned cart), profiling and loyalty programs, reviews, transaction tracking toward advertising platforms, the dual order/tax basis, wishlists and behavioral data. Cross-cutting topics remain in their dedicated areas and are referenced here by ID: consent on forms and guest mode at checkout (see G-05, G-07, G-11, G-13), pre-consent trackers and the banner (see Area C and Area D), channel security and session cookies (see F-01, F-06, F-07), third parties and transfers (see Area E), the exercise of rights (see Area H).

**Legal bases**: Art. 5(1)(b) GDPR (purpose limitation) [N1], Art. 5(1)(c) GDPR (minimization) [N1], Art. 5(1)(e) GDPR (storage limitation) [N1], Art. 5(2) GDPR (accountability) [N1], Art. 6(1)(a) GDPR (consent) [N1], Art. 6(1)(b) GDPR (contractual necessity, restrictive interpretation) [N1, S46], Art. 6(1)(c) GDPR (legal obligation) [N1], Art. 6(1)(f) GDPR (legitimate interest: necessity and balancing test) [N1], Art. 6(4) GDPR (compatibility of further processing) [N1], Art. 7 GDPR (conditions for consent: distinguishability, withdrawal, non-conditionality) [N1], Art. 11 GDPR (processing not requiring identification) [N1], Art. 12(6) GDPR (identification of the requester by means other than the account) [N1], Art. 17 GDPR (erasure) [N1], Art. 25 GDPR (protection by design and by default) [N1], Art. 32 GDPR (security of processing) [N1], Recitals 41 and 47 GDPR (foreseeability of the legal obligation, legitimate interest) [N1], Art. 122 of the Codice Privacy (Italian Privacy Code, D.lgs. 196/2003) (storage of and access to the terminal, basis for conversion tags) [N2], Art. 130 of the Codice Privacy (unsolicited communications and soft spam under paragraph 4) [N2], Art. 5(3) of Directive 2002/58/EC ePrivacy (consent for storing and reading on the terminal) [N3], CJEU case C-252/21 Meta v. Bundeskartellamt (contractual necessity in the strict sense) [S46], EDPB Recommendations 2/2025 on the legal basis for imposing account creation on e-commerce sites (adopted on 2-3 December 2025, in public consultation until 12 February 2026: used as interpretive guidance, not as binding law) [S46], EDPB Recommendations 02/2021 on the legal basis for storing credit card numbers for future purchases [S51], Conseil d'Etat, decision of 10 December 2020 on card saving [S51], CNIL (the French DPA), guidance and enforcement on e-commerce (card storage, account creation, prospecting) [S51]. Operational and breadth support from the corpus: reinforced protection of payment data and minimization [S10], requirements on consent, opt-out, retention and self-service erasure [S10, S11]. Statutory reference for accounting retention: art. 2220 of the Italian Civil Code and art. 22 of D.P.R. 600/1973 (ten years for accounting records and tax documents).

| L-04 | [COND: card saving for future purchases] Saving the payment datum for future reuse occurs only with active, specific consent separated from the transaction, collected with a non-pre-selected checkbox and revocable; never as a default setting | Saving the card for subsequent purchases is not necessary to the single transaction: it is further processing that requires an active choice. A pre-ticked box is not valid consent and saving cannot be the default. The standard must be applied uniformly, including toward EU customers in cross-border sales. Art. 6(1)(a) and Art. 7 GDPR (free, specific, distinguishable and revocable consent) [N1], EDPB Recommendations 02/2021 on the storage of card numbers and Conseil d'Etat 10 December 2020 [S51], uniform cross-border application [S51] | MANDATORY | HIGH | 🟡 | Checkout journey up to the payment step without completing it: verify that the card-saving option is a separate checkbox, not pre-selected and not mandatory to pay; flag if the card is saved by default or if consent is bundled with acceptance of the order [S51, S10] |
| L-05 | [COND: post-purchase email marketing] Promotional emails sent to customers on the basis of soft spam remain within the perimeter of Art. 130(4): same controller, products or services similar to those purchased, opt-out offered at the time the email is collected and in every subsequent send; any communication outside this perimeter requires consent | Soft spam allows reusing the email collected on the occasion of a sale to promote similar products, but only within strict cumulative conditions: it must concern one's own similar products, the data subject must be able to refuse already at collection and in every message, and the mere creation of an account without a purchase does not confer customer status. Third-party offers, unrelated product categories, or communications to those who have not purchased fall outside the exception and revert to the regime of prior consent. The opt-out mechanism is dealt with in G-11, non-conditionality in G-07. | L-06 | [COND: abandoned cart email] Sending abandoned-cart recovery emails is based on consent, not on soft spam | An abandoned cart is not a concluded sale: the very premise of the soft spam exception is missing, which requires a sale against which the email was collected. Soliciting someone who put products in the cart without purchasing is direct marketing to all intents and purposes and requires the data subject's prior consent; the address entered at checkout for an order never completed is not freely reusable for promotional purposes. Art. 6(1)(a) GDPR (consent) [N1], Art. 130 Codice Privacy (unsolicited communications, outside the paragraph 4 exception) [N2], Art. 5(1)(b) purpose limitation [N1] | MANDATORY | HIGH | 🟡 | Test with a disposable email controlled by the auditor: adding a product to the cart and entering the email at checkout without completing the order, then observing whether recovery emails arrive and verifying the declared basis; flag if the cart email is sent absent consent [S10] |
| L-07 | [COND: loyalty program or profiling] Tracking activity for loyalty building, personalized content, or a loyalty program is based on consent and on an active choice, with consent separate from the purchase process, and is not imposed through the account | Profiling the customer's behavior and building loyalty through tracking is not necessary to the sale and does not fall within the reasonable expectations tied to a purchase: it requires consent and, when it involves reading or writing on the terminal, ePrivacy consent. The account cannot be the lever to impose profiling: consent-based services (history, personalized offers, loyalty) must be separated from the core of the purchase, so that those who do not join are not disadvantaged. | L-08 | [COND: product reviews] Publishing buyer reviews rests on a defined legal basis and minimizes the data exposed: no full surname, email, or unnecessary identifying data made public, with notice about the use of the contribution | A review ties an opinion to an identifiable person and makes it public: the basis (author's consent or balanced legitimate interest) must be identified and what is shown limited to the necessary minimum, as a rule a name or pseudonym, never email or contact data. If the shop marks reviews as verified, it must be able to demonstrate the link to a real purchase and inform the data subject of the use of their contribution. | L-09 | [COND: conversion tags or transaction tracking] Purchase events and transaction data (order value, products, email or phone, even in hashed form) sent to advertising platforms (Meta CAPI, Google Ads enhanced conversions, server-side tracking) fire only after consent and are declared in the privacy notice | E-commerce conversion tracking transmits to a third-party advertiser strongly identifying data tied to a purchase: hashing the email or the phone does not make them anonymous, they remain pseudonymized personal data. Writing or reading on the terminal that feeds these tags is subject to ePrivacy consent, and transferring the transaction datum to the third party requires a basis and transparency. It is an e-commerce specialization of the tracker regime (see Area C): the risk is that the conversion tag fires on order completion, ignoring the refusal expressed in the banner. | L-10 | Order data and tax documents are governed as distinct bases and retentions: order data on a contractual basis are deleted or anonymized at the end of the need (fulfillment, warranty, limitation period), while the invoice and accounting documents remain on a legal obligation, in a separate archive with restricted access and for the tax purpose alone | It is the classic e-commerce error: using the tax retention obligation to keep the entire customer profile indefinitely in an active database. The legal obligation requires retaining specific documents (invoices, accounting records) for the statutory term, typically ten years under art. 2220 of the Italian Civil Code and art. 22 D.P.R. 600/1973, not keeping all order data accessible and reusable. The two purposes must be separated: contractual basis with its own time for order management, legal-obligation basis with restricted access for accounting alone. Art. 6(1)(b) and 6(1)(c) GDPR (distinct bases) [N1], Art. 5(1)(b) purpose limitation [N1], Art. 5(1)(e) storage limitation [N1], Recital 41 GDPR (foreseeability and proportionality of the obligation) [N1], separation of the tax archive [S46] | MANDATORY | HIGH | 🔴 | Documentary check at intake: existence of a retention matrix that distinguishes, for the same customer, the order datum (contractual basis, erasure at end of need) from the tax document (legal obligation, separate archive with restricted access); flag if the only criterion applied is ten-year retention over the whole profile [S46] |
| L-11 | [COND: customer account available] Inactive or orphaned accounts do not remain indefinitely in an active database: a period is defined after which they are deleted or anonymized, and the order history in the account remains a service on distinct consent from the core of the purchase | Keeping in an active database accounts that no one uses anymore violates storage limitation and widens the surface exposed to a takeover or a breach. An inactivity period must be set, beyond which the account is closed, with erasure or anonymization of the data not covered by another basis (tax documents follow L-10). The order history viewable in the account is an additional service on consent, not a reason to retain the profile indefinitely. Art. 5(1)(e) storage limitation [N1], Art. 17 erasure [N1], Art. 6(1)(a) for the history as a consent-based service [N1], management of inactive or orphaned accounts [S46] | RECOMMENDED | MEDIUM | 🔴 | Verification at intake of the policy on inactive accounts (time threshold, erasure or anonymization action) and review of the account settings for the presence of self-service erasure (see H-05); flag if no criterion exists for decommissioning dormant accounts [S10, S11] |
| L-12 | [COND: wishlist or personalized recommendations] Wishlists, wish lists, and browsing history used for recommendations or profiling have a defined legal basis (consent, or legitimate interest with documented balancing), notice, and the possibility to object | The wishlist and behavioral history reveal preferences and purchase intentions: using them to profile or generate recommendations is processing that must be explicitly grounded, not inferred from use of the site. Personalized recommendations do not in themselves justify the account, and a consumer at checkout does not expect to conclude a contract for personalization. If legitimate interest is invoked, a documented balancing test is needed; if behavioral tracking writes on the terminal, ePrivacy consent is needed. Art. 6(1)(a) or 6(1)(f) GDPR with balancing [N1], Art. 5(3) ePrivacy Directive for tracked history [N3], Art. 13 GDPR (information) [N1], EDPB on personalized recommendations and the account [S46] | RECOMMENDED | MEDIUM | 🟡 | Review of the wishlist and recommendation functions and cross-reference with detected trackers (see Area C): verify that the profiling use of the history rests on consent or on a legitimate interest with balancing documented at intake, and that the notice declares the purpose and the right to object [S46] |

**Engagement note**: checks L-04, L-06 and L-09 require a checkout journey or a test order. They must be run only with the client's authorization for the assessment, using synthetic email and data controlled by the auditor and never real third-party payment data: L-04 stops before completing payment, L-06 abandons the cart, L-09 completes a test order only if the client sets up a zero-cost product or code and confirms in writing. Checks L-01, L-02, L-05, L-10 and L-11 are wholly or partly documentary: the scan provides the technical evidence (purchase flow, forms, tags, data shown), the intake provides the organizational part (necessity assessments, retention matrix, account policies). The strongest finding arises from the gap between what the shop actually does and what the client documents as having decided.

**Client questions (Area L)**

**Account and services without registration**
- Do you sell online? Does the checkout allow purchasing as a guest or do you impose the creation of an account to buy? If the account is mandatory, what limited purpose justifies it and where is the assessment retained?
- Do you offer subscriptions or access to offers reserved for a closed community? With what access requirements (invitation, referral, verified status)?
- Returns, exchanges, warranties, order tracking, and modifications before shipping: can they be handled without an account, via form, email, or a specific link?

**Payments**
- How do you collect payment? Which PSP do you use and are the card fields hosted by the PSP (iframe, redirect) or entered on your domain? Does the PAN transit or persist on your systems?
- Do you offer card saving for future purchases? How do you collect consent and with what default?

**Marketing and conversion tracking**
- Do you send promotional emails to customers after purchase? Do you limit them to products similar to those purchased and do you offer the opt-out already at collection and in every send?
- Do you send abandoned-cart recovery emails? On what legal basis?
- Do you use conversion tags or transaction tracking toward Meta, Google Ads, or other platforms (including server-side, CAPI, enhanced conversions)? Do these tags respect the banner choice, including refusal?

**Profiling, loyalty, and reviews**
- Do you have a loyalty program or do you profile customers? Is the consent to tracking separate from registration and from the order?
- Do you publish buyer reviews? Which author data do you show and how do you verify that the review comes from a real purchase?
- Do you offer wishlists, wish lists, or personalized recommendations? On what legal basis do you use browsing history and how do you inform the data subject?

**Retention and data lifecycle**
- How do you distinguish the retention of order data from that of tax documents? Is there a retention matrix with distinct bases and times for the same customer?
- Do customer accounts have a decommissioning policy for inactivity? After how long do you delete or anonymize them?
